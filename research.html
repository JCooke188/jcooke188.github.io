Research Interests

My primary area of interest lies at the intersection of turbulence and geophysical flows. I employ numerical modeling to aid in understanding the physics of the world around us, and aim to apply my knowledge to help improve the quality of life of those that reside there.

Current Work

I am currently a Postdoctoral Fellow on the Understanding Gulf Ocean Systems project to better understand the mechansims of the Loop Current (LC) and Loop Current Eddies (LCE) in the Gulf of Mexico. My work focuses on identifying the role of deep (> 2000 m depth) cyclones in the modification of the upper LC and shedding of LCE. We use publicly available HYCOM results due to the availability of the long time-series. Our future work will apply the learnings from this study to discern why some members of ensemble forecasts perform better than others, with an emphasis on analyzing the deep flow characteristics.

Past Projects

Internal Boundary Layers

I investigated the development of an internal boundary layer (IBL) encoutnering a roughness transition within an atmospheric boundary layer (ABL) at White Sands National Park in New Mexico, USA, using wall-modeled large-eddy simulation. This study led to the discovery of a self-similarity of the structure of turbulence in IBLs, as the new length-scales of the flow are dictated by the IBLs growth. This work was published in PNAS.

In a follow-up to this work, I investigated how these changes to turbulence influenced sweep and ejection events through an expanded quandrant analysis, with consequences for sediment transport, and ultimately, the formation and migration of the dunes which influence the flow. This work has been published in JGR Atmospheres.

As this work was completed within the context of an ABL, much of the near-wall information was unavailable due to the high Reynolds number. So, using available experimental datasets of smooth-to-rough, rough-to-smooth, and rough-to-rougher (and vice versa), a study was conducted to gauge the universality of this IBL-based scaling. From the data, I determined the length- and velocity-scales formed by the developing IBL allow for the collapse of mean velocity and velocity defect profiles within. This work is currently under review, but a preprint is available here.

Low Reynolds Number Aerodynamics

I conducted research on the influence of surface roughness on propellers desigend for low Reynolds number flows, to be applied to micro aerial vehicle (MAV) flight. Using a large-eddy simulation flow solver, we determined the surface roughness enhances the vortex structures, particularly the leading-edge vortex (LEV). The LEV is known to augment lift at low Reynolds numbers, and this was reflected by an increase in thrust coefficient for the rough propeller, compared to a baseline smooth propeller. This work was published in Physics of Fluids.
